# Karagiozis Crisis Runner — Branding and Marketing

## Positioning

The project should be positioned first as a stylized satirical action-runner with a unique Greek shadow-theater identity, and only second as a political game. On PC storefronts especially, the hook has to be instantly legible in one line, because Steam is extremely crowded: around 20,000 projects were expected to release on Steam in 2025, and only a small fraction were projected to cross $1 million in gross revenue.

This means the brand cannot rely on "Greek politics" alone. The commercial pitch needs to combine three things in one sentence: a clear genre, a striking cultural identity, and a memorable gameplay hook.

Recommended positioning line:

> A shadow-theater-inspired 2D action-runner through a country collapsing under debt, spectacle, and false hope.

## Brand Pillars

1. Greek cultural identity — shadow-theater silhouettes, recognizable urban and political symbolism, and a trickster protagonist rooted in folklore rather than generic indie aesthetics.
2. Satirical anger — witty, bitter, absurd, and emotionally charged, not academic or preachy.
3. Readable arcade action — the game must look fun in motion even to someone who does not understand the references.
4. Diaspora nostalgia and trauma — collective memory for Greeks who lived through or left during the crisis.

## Audience

Think in layers, not one market.

Primary: Greeks in Greece who understand the symbolism; Greeks abroad who feel nostalgia, anger, or curiosity; Greek meme-media audiences who share political-cultural jokes quickly.

Secondary: indie players who like unusual cultural settings; players drawn to satirical or politically themed games; streamers and gaming press who respond to a bold premise and strong visual identity.

Tertiary: people with no Greek context who simply find the art style, pace, and setup intriguing.

The project works best when it lands at all three layers. If it only works for Greeks who know every reference, its ceiling is low.

## Naming Strategy

The title should not be too local, too long, or too opaque to non-Greek players. It should foreground either the protagonist or the crisis-runner fantasy. Candidates:

- Karagiozis: Crisis Run
- Karagiozis and the Euro Nightmare
- Shadow of Austerity
- Debt Run: Karagiozis
- Karagiozis: Last Referendum

The final choice should be easy to pronounce in English, instantly evocative in a thumbnail, and still meaningful to Greek players.

## Visual Identity

The visual identity is the strongest differentiator. Avoid looking like a generic platformer; blend shadow-puppet silhouettes, exaggerated political caricature, and readable arcade motion. Key rules:

- Silhouette-first character design so screenshots stay recognizable at small size.
- Bright colours reserved for symbolic elements: fire, euro projectiles, tokens, propaganda, false prosperity, boss attacks.
- Minimal, bold UI; the world carries most of the storytelling.
- One signature image motif per faction or era (fake-prosperity gold, austerity grey, propaganda green, euro-blue hazards).

## Messaging Strategy

Do not lead with "a game about Greek politicians" — that narrows reach and invites platform friction. Public-facing copy emphasizes a crisis-ridden country, satire and folklore, a trickster hero, and frantic 2D action. Angles:

- "A satirical runner inspired by Greek shadow theater."
- "Run through a collapsing country built from debt, spectacle, and absurdity."
- "Use an elastic hand to swing, strike, and survive the crisis."

Internal Greek-channel messaging can be more direct and culturally loaded; the main store page, trailer text, and festival pitches stay one layer more universal.

## PC Store Strategy

Focus on discoverability, wishlists, and identity rather than assuming high raw volume. Steam has major spending power but brutal release volume and visibility competition. Priorities:

1. Capsule art — protagonist silhouette, the hand mechanic, one dominant symbolic antagonist or hazard.
2. Short description — one sentence: genre + setting + mechanic.
3. GIFs/screenshots — running, hand extension, symbolic hazards, one boss moment.
4. Demo timing — a small but polished demo to collect wishlists and test response.
5. Tag discipline — 2D Platformer, Runner, Action, Satire, Story Rich, only where justified.

Early success on PC is measured in traction signals, not sales: wishlist growth, demo completion, press pickup, organic clips, strong comments from Greek and non-Greek players alike.

## Mobile Strategy

Mobile is the strongest route for mass Greek reach because internet and mobile usage in Greece are very high — ELSTAT reported 88.7% of households with internet access in 2025, 89.2% of people aged 16–74 using the internet, and 95.0% of internet users accessing it via mobile or smartphone. That makes mobile the natural platform for rapid cultural spread, but not automatically for premium conversion; it would need very sharp onboarding, strong five-second readability, and heavy shareability.

Pricing options for mobile: a free demo with paid unlock; a low upfront premium price (around €1.99–€2.99); or free with no predatory monetization plus a supporter upgrade. A straight €2–5 premium launch is an upside scenario, not the default plan. This is consistent with the platform decision: PC/Steam first, mobile considered later.

## Greece-Specific Marketing Channels

The biggest edge is cultural virality inside Greece and the diaspora. The Greek media environment is heavily digital and fragmented, with high social-media use for news and discovery, which supports meme-led distribution. Best-fit channels:

- Greek meme/news outlets (Luben-style channels).
- Greek gaming creators on YouTube and TikTok.
- Diaspora Facebook groups and Greek expat communities.
- Reddit communities tied to Greece, gaming, and indie development.
- Greek podcasts and cultural-commentary channels.
- Short-form clips built around visual jokes and boss reveals.

Marketing content should travel as clips, screenshots, and one-line jokes rather than long explanations.

## Trailer Strategy

A 30–45 second reveal trailer:

1. Immediate hook — protagonist silhouette and the arm mechanic in the first 2–3 seconds.
2. Visual escalation — fake prosperity, collapse, euro hazards, boss attacks.
3. Signature satire beat — an ally betrayal or a symbolic fake-win moment.
4. Strong final stinger — the doomed continuation / endless-era reveal.
5. Call to action — wishlist, follow, or play demo.

Sell emotion and identity before systems depth.

## Content Strategy

Lightweight and repeatable for a solo dev.

Pre-announcement: concept-art snippets, character silhouette tests, one hand-mechanic clip, polls on titles or motifs.

Announcement: one reveal trailer, Steam page live, a short concept post in universal terms, Greek and English versions of the main post.

Development: weekly or biweekly short clips, boss teasers, graybox-to-art-pass before/afters, "Greek players will understand this" micro-posts, occasional longer devlogs (clips matter more).

Demo: outreach to Greek creators first, then indie press and streamers; a clip package for TikTok, Reels, Shorts, and X.

## Community Strategy

Build two overlapping audiences: a Greek-language cultural hype layer and an English-language indie-curiosity layer. Practical rule: every major asset exists in both Greek and English where possible — trailer subtitles, key-art posts, short descriptions, demo announcements. Let Greeks feel ownership while keeping the project legible to the wider indie market.

## Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Why it matters | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Too local | Non-Greek players do not connect | Lead with game feel and visuals before specific references |
| Too political | Storefronts, creators, or players disengage | Use allegory, satire, and clear caricature, not literal claims |
| Too niche on PC | Steam visibility is hard | Focus on capsule, trailer, demo, festival-style positioning |
| Hard to monetize on mobile | Downloads may not convert to paid | Free demo + unlock or a low-cost premium model |
| Brand confusion | Players cannot tell if it is comedy, rage, or arcade | One consistent tone: bitter satire with fast readable action |

## Launch Path

1. Build a polished PC demo first.
2. Create the Steam page early and gather wishlists.
3. Use Greek channels to test cultural traction.
4. Use English-language indie channels to test broader curiosity.
5. Only then decide whether to build the mobile version as the real reach play.

This validates concept, tone, and mechanic before committing to full mobile production in a crowded market.

## First-Year Goals

Milestones, not fantasies.

Strong early indicators: 5,000–20,000 Steam wishlists before launch; noticeable Greek social sharing; a few creator videos or press mentions; a demo completion rate that suggests the core loop is fun; organic comments from non-Greek players that the game still looks appealing.

Stretch outcomes: a mobile version gains strong local press and meme-channel pickup; Greek creators make the game a conversation piece beyond the usual indie circle; the brand grows into a recognizable cultural IP rather than a one-off joke.

## Final Brand Rule

The brand should always be culturally specific, emotionally real, mechanically simple, visually memorable, and publicly legible beyond Greece. If any new idea makes the project less readable, more legally risky, or more dependent on insider knowledge, it weakens the brand rather than strengthens it.
